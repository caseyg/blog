Things I have read that swirl around my mind.

The privilege of toys and who gets to play what and when with who.

Decolonization in teaching and learning -- the notion that we, teachers successful within the dominant paradigm, will come "emancipate" the "lesser" masses is... awfully presumptuous. I've got my own colonialist mentalities and unexamined privilege; even if I'm a deaf minority woman, I've chosen to train so I can hold my own in a white man's world as measured by able-bodied, Anglocentric metrics, and I need to be aware at all times that this isn't the pinnacle of achievement that everyone should be "freed" to climb. Mandates of English as a lingua franca aren't just unfair, they don't make economic sense) Multivocality is similar. Talk in your tongue, stand on your turf; assimilation, not subordination, is the fullest kind of conquest that there is -- be who you are, where you are, and bridge from that.

Seeing my own Impostor Syndrome and still having it. I've always thought and written rhizomatically about my research, and it was a blessing I didn't know that wasn't "normal" -- because when I found out, I went through a doubt drought where I had to constantly remind myself to trust my gut. I don't need external justification, but it still helps, even if I don't want it to -- reading other post-ists lets me know I can cite them and be validated by an academic world I've chosen to care about. Explaining and justifying yourself constantly can be exhausting.

Plenty of good material on open access and transparency. Ana showed me the Open Science Framework, which is linked to the Center for Open Science and a project to reproduce experiments to check validity of journals. It's hard to sort through the tangle of "openwashing" in academia and government; some people throw around buzzwords like "transparency" and "participation" without understanding what they are and how they're linked. Much of my work -- research, writing, teaching, activism -- is about transparency, but I also agree with Aaron that transparency is a means and not an end, not a magic wand. And what do ethnographic researches make of this?

Deliberate practice on creating space. This discipline of doing less, of making room and time to breathe.
